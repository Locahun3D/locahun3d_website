Using 3DGS as an "Aerial Shot" in
UE5 with the XGRIDs SDK

You don't need a drone to get an aerial shot in a no-fly zone. Kou Nakamura, founder of Locahun 3D, tested a workflow that imports a 3DGS scan into UE5, places a virtual camera at an angle you could never actually shoot, and then lets AI handle only the lighting — along with an honest look at where the technique breaks down today.

01 The problem: what a 3D scan can't reach, it can't show

With 3D scanning, anywhere the scan didn't reach simply comes out missing. The rooftop of a high-rise, for instance, isn't somewhere you can walk to with a handheld scanner. Flying a drone would solve that — except most of central Tokyo is a no-fly zone, so shooting from above isn't even permitted in the first place. In city-center location scouting, this exact situation comes up constantly: you want the aerial shot, but there's no legal way to get equipment up there.

02 The fix: import 3DGS into UE5 with the XGRIDs SDK and fly a virtual camera

The workaround: import the Shibuya scramble crossing 3DGS data into UE5 via the XGRIDs SDK, in Lcc2 format. Once the scene exists as 3DGS, everything downstream becomes a game-engine problem. The camera is no longer bound by physics — it can sit at rooftop height, or directly above an intersection, moved anywhere you like. The ground-level scan data stays the foundation; only the aerial angle itself comes from a virtual camera.

The key pointThe foundation is still an actual 3DGS scan. Nothing about the background is generated from scratch by AI — what's been added on top of a real, scan-based point cloud is simply a camera move that would be physically impossible to shoot. That's the whole premise of this technique.

03 Changing lighting and season with AI — the prompt is just "make it night"

Once the framing is locked in the UE5 viewport, you just screenshot that view and hand it to an image-generation AI (GPT). The prompt is a short instruction like "make it night" or "make it winter." Composition, camera angle, and building placement — everything that needs to be accurate — is already locked in on the UE5 side (i.e., the real scan data), so all that's left for the AI to do is the lighting and seasonal atmosphere.

04 Why this works

  • Guaranteed framing, direction, and accuracy — camera work and composition are locked in on the UE5 side, avoiding the "not quite what I asked for" problem common with pure AI generation.
  • A major cut in shooting cost and time — no on-site reshoot, permit application, or equipment logistics; the framing gets decided entirely at a desk.
  • Unlimited retries — once the scene exists as 3DGS, camera position and time of day can be redone as many times as needed.
  • Reinventing the shoot itself — shifting from "go on location and shoot" to "move a camera inside data you've already scanned." Also works for video (camera animation).

05 Current limits: signage, text, and man-made detail break down

To be upfront: this is a conditional technique, not a universal one. Signage text and fine man-made details — trains, road signs, and similar structures — currently break down under AI data completion. Looking at the actual generated images, you can see "ghost" structures that shouldn't exist, and a train-like structure incorrectly generated where there shouldn't be one.

Signage and fine detail are, roughly speaking, going to be missing with the current technique. Controlling for that in detail and delivering at a quality fit for public release is what makes this a professional job rather than a demo. In practice, that means designing the shots that are prone to breaking down with a reshoot or manual touch-up already planned in. The precision of AI data completion itself is something I'd like to see keep improving.

What we know todayThis isn't a universal solution for every shot. It suits framings where signage, text, and man-made objects don't end up in frame, or shots where some breakdown is acceptable. It is not, at least for now, suited to a shot where legible signage text matters.

06 Summary: the advantage of always routing through one real scan

The core idea here is that the shoot is never fully handed over to AI generation — it always routes through one real scan (3DGS). Composition, framing, and accuracy stay anchored to the scan data and a virtual camera; AI is only ever responsible for finishing the look. That division of labor is exactly what keeps the breakdown in check while making a physically impossible angle usable in practice.
